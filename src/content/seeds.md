### The Cost of Hesitation (Seed → Draft)
**Seed planted**: 2026-03-18
**Seed text**: "Many leaders get stuck - and often because of extenuating factors - in the mire of indecision. But ultimately clear decision-making, communication and action defines the leader. If a leader therefore pauses for too long, there is a cost to this. The basic cost is of course just time; time spent not doing something is time wasted. And on this time there can be other costs, like the cost of running a business, paying salaries, etc... Or the cost of opportunity, of not doing something - not in favor of something else - and doing nothing instead. I'm not against waiting; sometimes waiting is indeed the answer. I just think that waiting has a cost, which I observe many leaders do not take into account."

### Organizational Structure: Autonomy vs Hierarchy
**Seed planted**: 2026-03-22
**Seed expanded**: 2026-03-23
**Seed text**: "I'm thinking about leadership and organizational structure. What is the optimal organizational structure, and what are principles for this? It obviously depends on the type of organization, and the mission. Some missions are inherently hierarchical in terms of grand strategy, while having great autonomy in terms of on-the-ground tactics, like military. Other missions benefit greatly from having autonomy throughout the organization, like Spotify's famous 'Tribes' structure. I don't think there is a perfect one, but I'd like to use the post to just ruminate over what I personally prefer, which leans toward having autonomy throughout the organization, while recognizing that hierarchy is a necessary thing for decision-making tie-breaking."

**Additional insights (2026-03-23)**:
"The strength of the organization is actually built around trust, regardless of the type of structure. A structure is ultimately to fulfill a function; optimizing for decisive decision-making versus facilitating fast decision-making, for example. Note that a strong team is strong regardless of the structure, as long as they are together. This is also built on trust. We can refer to Google's Project Aristotle, about psychological safety, which proved this to be the strongest predictor of team success.

So perhaps the question for an organization looking to restructure is this: where are the clusters of strong trust, and where are the weak links? One option is for the organization to optimize to separate the weak links; keep the strong teams together, and shuffle the weak ones in an attempt to create new strong teams. This is likely to create attrition though. Another option is to form teams centred around the strong links, allowing them to become 'glue' for the organization. This works, for as long as the strong links stay. These strong links are often elevated to managers, which is another sort of problem if they are not really suited, or desiring to be managers.

I don't think this is a necessarily one step process; you cannot really expect reshuffling without some friction. The game that must be played is to reshuffle into a position where stronger links can be formed. So, a little like arranging a garden, one must consider how to move as few things as possible (moving too many kills the plants), to establish an ecosystem that nurtures further growth."

**Potential title**: "The Structure of Success: Finding Balance Between Autonomy and Hierarchy"
**Alternative title**: "Trust as Architecture: Building Organizations That Work"
**Key themes**: Leadership, Organizational Design, Decision-making, Team Structure, Trust, Psychological Safety, Google's Project Aristotle
