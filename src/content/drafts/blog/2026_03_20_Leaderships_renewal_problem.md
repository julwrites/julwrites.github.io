The renewal of leaders has been a particular interest of mine in recent years. I'd like to say it's because I matured, but the truth is more pragmatic: I've observed and participated in organizations where incumbent leaders consistently fail to replace themselves successfully.

Before taking a stance, I want to acknowledge that succession is a complex concept. For it to succeed, three conditions must align:

1. **Capable candidates** who have demonstrated sufficient capability, loyalty, and future potential.
2. **Trust and respect** between the incumbent leader and these candidates.
3. **Willingness to relinquish**—a real desire for the incumbent leader to step down.

Where does it fail?

At first glance, the incumbent leader seems to blame, but it's not that simple. The only part of this equation the incumbent leader actually controls is themselves.

Certainly, pride keeps many leaders from stepping down—we see this everywhere today—but it's only one part of the problem. Perhaps it's the least of the three.

**Can an incumbent leader identify suitable candidates and groom them until they're ready?**

Even with the best intentions, identifying, engaging, and grooming candidates to succeed you is not simple. You may find you've only made them capable enough to leave rather than succeed you.

**Can the candidate wait patiently and humbly for the incumbent leader to pass the baton?**

If the candidate desires your position, it's not simple to make them ready while keeping them humble. The same pride that keeps an incumbent leader in power drives another to take power.

## The Successor's Paradox

We often treat succession as a search for a "suitable" candidate, as if we're looking for a missing puzzle piece. But the reality of high-performance leadership introduces a fundamental paradox: **the very traits that make someone a great successor also make them the most likely to leave.**

A truly driven successor is defined by two traits: **Passion** and **Opinion**.

### 1. The Entropy of Passion
Passion is contagious, but it's also corrosive in a mediocre environment. A driven, passionate leader cannot coexist for long with a dispassionate organization. If the organization's quality has stagnated, the successor won't wait for the baton—they'll leave to find (or build) an environment that matches their frequency. Organizational quality isn't just a recruiting tool; it's the "containment field" for succession.

### 2. Ownership vs. Stewardship
Strong opinions drive a leader toward **Ownership** rather than **Stewardship**. If I'm opinionated about the "How" and the "Why," why would I settle for merely stewarding someone else's legacy? The successor craves both the *authority* to act and the *autonomy* to create.

This is the ultimate test for the incumbent: Can you provide a mission so large and an alignment so deep that the successor believes starting their own organization would actually be a *step down* in impact?

## The Servant vs. The King

A deeper distinction determines whether a successor stays or leaves: the posture of the incumbent leader.

The disciple-making leader, like Christ, is **a servant to all, not a king**.

This is the ultimate anchor for the succession question. An opinionated successor will only remain in an organization where the top leader is a **Servant** who provides **Mission Gravity**, rather than a **King** who demands **Compliance**.

- **The King** builds a court of loyal subjects who execute his will. He selects for compliance. The capable successor comes, sees the castle the King has built, and realizes they don't want to inherit the throne—they want to build their own kingdom.
- **The Servant** builds a field where workers are welcome. He provides vision and resources, not decrees. The capable successor comes, sees the harvest the Servant has cultivated, and realizes the *field* is bigger than any one worker. They stay because the mission transcends the man.

## The Solution: Stewardship over Legacy

Ultimately, the only variable an incumbent leader truly controls is their willingness to let go. This requires a fundamental mindset shift—one that aligns with the highest levels of leadership: moving from a focus on **Legacy** to a focus on **Stewardship**.

When we focus on legacy, we're often trying to preserve ourselves through the organization. We seek a successor who's a clone, someone who will protect what we built exactly as we built it. This creates the "loyalty paradox," where we accidentally select for compliance over capability.

Stewardship, however, recognizes that the organization doesn't belong to the leader. A steward's job is to prepare the "house" for the next occupant and then hand over the keys. This means accepting the risk that the next leader will do things differently. It means trusting the mission enough to let it evolve beyond our own shadow.

The ultimate measure of leadership isn't what we built while we were there—it's whether what we built is healthy enough to survive our departure. Renewal isn't about finding a perfect replacement; it's about the courage to exit and the humility to trust the next steward.

---

Most leadership renewals end either in the death of the organization or in a painful transition as leaders vie for the top.

I believe the only successful kind of renewal happens with a true servant leader.
